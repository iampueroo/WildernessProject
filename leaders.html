What causes the emission of short bursts of light from imploding bubbles in a liquid when excited by sound?: When a bubble formed by sound traveling through liquid begins collapsing, an environment of high pressure and temperature forms in the interior, causing the ionization of whatever gas is inside of it. The amount is so small, however, the bubble remains at an approximate 1-micrometer diameter. As the electrons of both ionized and neutral gas atoms interact within the bubble, electron waves of the respective particles continue fluctuating to maximum and minimum amplitudes. Given that the minimum amplitude also represents the point of lowest energy, when the two waves are superimposed at this lowest point, the pressure and temperature immediately drop. Thus, free electrons released by gaseous atoms in the process of ionizing are reincorporated into the electron shell of ionized atoms. Despite the relatively small drop in pressure, and therefore temperature, within the bubble, the large value of re-ionization energy is enough to excite the electron to a point where it releases a brief light pulse.
